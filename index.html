PROJECT 01 · TATA GROUP SIMULATION
Blinkit Sales & Performance Analysis
Real Business Question
"Blinkit has 8,500+ SKUs across multiple outlet tiers — but inventory spend is not tied to actual revenue contribution. Which outlets are being starved of investment despite generating strong returns, and which SKUs are silently consuming working capital without earning their place?"
Executive Summary
Blinkit's ₹80.3L revenue was concentrated in Tier 1 outlets receiving the highest spend — yet Tier 3 outlets generated ₹31.7L (39.3%) on the lowest investment in the network. The business was accidentally starving its highest-potential market. Decision: reallocate operational budget from Tier 1 to Tier 3 and cut bottom 25% of SKUs in 30 days.
Problem8,500+ SKUs across 3 outlet tiers with no data on which inventory earns vs. which drains warehouse cost and working capital. Budget allocated by assumption. No centralized performance view. Management decisions delayed by fragmented reports.
ActionQueried all 12 product categories via SQL — ranked by revenue contribution per outlet tier. Python EDA segmented ₹80.3L revenue by fat content preference. Identified top 5 revenue-driving categories. Revenue-per-SKU efficiency metric calculated to expose deadweight inventory.
OutcomeTier 3 outlets: ₹31.7L revenue (39.3% of total) on the lowest operational spend. Underinvestment gap confirmed. Low Fat segment: ₹28.4L in untargeted revenue — zero dedicated SKU coverage. Top 312 SKUs of 1,248 drive 79.6% of revenue — 75% of catalog is deadweight.
STOP: Stocking bottom 25% of SKUs in Tier 3 low-traffic zones. Stop equal promo spend across all outlet tiers — Tier 3 earns 39.3% on the least spend. Stop ignoring the Low Fat segment — ₹28.4L sits untargeted.
Who benefits: Operations team (SKU rationalization roadmap) · Supply Chain (warehouse allocation by tier) · Category Managers (Low Fat SKU brief) · Management (budget reallocation decision)
Trade-off: Reallocating budget from Tier 1 to Tier 3 may reduce Tier 1 order volume 8–12% in the transition quarter before Tier 3 scales up.
Failure condition: Strategy fails if Tier 3 last-mile delivery infrastructure cannot scale within 90 days. Mitigation: Pilot in 2 high-revenue Tier 3 zones first — validate logistics capacity before full reallocation.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Freeze new SKU additions — audit bottom 25% of 1,248 SKUs against 6-month velocity data. Pull non-performers from Tier 3 warehouses within 30 days.
Priority 2: Shift ₹12–15L operational budget from over-invested Tier 1 to Tier 3 top 5 categories: Fruits, Snacks, Dairy, Household, Beverages.
Priority 3: Launch dedicated Low Fat SKU line — ₹28.4L segment is untargeted. A competitor entering this space first makes this analysis irrelevant.
Watch: Strategy collapses if Tier 3 logistics cannot scale in 90 days — pilot 2 zones before full rollout.

PROJECT 02 · ACCENTURE SIMULATION
Customer Shopping Behavior Analysis
Real Business Question
"Our marketing budget is spread equally across all customer segments and seasons — but conversion rates vary wildly. Which customer segments are actually driving revenue, which seasons are we over-spending in, and why are promo campaigns not delivering consistent ROI?"
Executive Summary
Marketing spend was distributed equally across all customer segments with no evidence that most of them converted at acceptable ROI. SQL analysis on 3,900+ records proved that promo users already spent 18–22% more — the business was wasting budget convincing non-converters instead of rewarding proven ones. Decision: redirect 70% of promo budget to male 25–45 segment immediately.
ProblemBlanket marketing campaigns with no ROI evidence per segment. Promo budgets spread across all age groups and seasons equally. Seasonal demand patterns guessed, not measured. Weekly management reports produced manually — no real-time visibility.
ActionSegmented 3,900+ records across 12 product categories using SQL. Ran cohort-level discount analysis comparing order values between promo and non-promo users. Analyzed purchase trends by gender, age, subscription status, and season. Replaced manual weekly reports with live Power BI dashboard.
OutcomeMale buyers aged 25–45: highest AOV cohort across all 12 categories. Promo users spent 18–22% more per order — confirmed across 3,900+ transactions. Q4: peak demand. Spring: sharpest revenue drop. Seasonal misalignment exposed — inventory and ad spend were not tracking actual buying cycles.
STOP: Blanket discounts to all segments — low-value users are consuming budget that should go to proven converters. Stop equal campaign spend across seasons — Q4 needs concentration, Spring needs reduction.
Who benefits: Marketing team (segment targeting brief) · CRM managers (promo tier structure) · Growth team (seasonal budget allocation) · Inventory planners (Q4 procurement lead time)
Trade-off: Redirecting promo budget away from low-value segments may reduce short-term order volume 5–8% in first 30 days before top-segment lift compensates.
Failure condition: Strategy fails if promo-driven customers don't convert to full-price within 60 days after discount removal. Mitigation: Retain 20% of promo budget as a re-engagement hold for 60 days — cut only if conversion rate doesn't lift.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Redirect 70% of promo budget to male 25–45 segment — highest AOV cohort confirmed across 12 categories.
Priority 2: Build Q4 inventory 45 days earlier — demand is predictable from data. Stop last-minute procurement based on assumption.
Priority 3: Kill Spring campaigns entirely — reallocate that budget to Q4 surge window where ROI is 3x higher.
Watch: If promo removal causes non-converters to drop off but top-segment lift doesn't materialise in 60 days — restore partial discount for top segment only.

PROJECT 03
Hotel Booking Analysis — Cancellation & Revenue Leakage
Real Business Question
"37% of our confirmed bookings are cancelling before check-in — and we're currently treating a 90-day advance booking the same as a same-day walk-in. Where is the revenue leaking, which customer profiles cancel most, and what policy change would stop the bleeding without reducing booking volume?"
Executive Summary
37% of 119,390 confirmed bookings cancelled before check-in — all with zero deposit obligation. Bookings made 61–90 days ahead cancelled at 2× the rate of last-minute bookings. The hotel was holding ₹ revenue on paper that evaporated. Decision: enforce 30% non-refundable deposit for all 60+ day advance bookings immediately.
Problem37% cancellation rate across 119,390 bookings — current policy treats a 90-day advance booking the same as a same-day walk-in. Zero deposit on high-risk long lead-time bookings. Revenue forecasting inaccurate. Peak season pricing not aligned to actual demand.
ActionPython EDA (Pandas, Seaborn, Matplotlib) on 119,390 booking records. Analysed cancellation patterns by lead time window, hotel type, customer origin, room type, and season. Built cancellation probability by lead time band. Identified high-risk booking profiles.
Outcome61–90 day advance bookings: 2× cancellation rate of last-minute bookings — paying zero deposit. City hotels: 41.7% cancel rate vs Resort: 27.8% — two different retention problems. July–August: 3× baseline demand. Peak season pricing not capturing available willingness-to-pay.
STOP: Applying same cancellation policy to all booking windows. Stop flat pricing in July–August — demand is 3× baseline, pricing isn't. Stop ignoring the city vs resort distinction — they need separate retention strategies.
Who benefits: Revenue management team (deposit policy change) · Pricing team (dynamic surge model) · Front office ops (separate playbooks by hotel type)
Trade-off: Enforcing 30% deposit for 60+ day bookings may reduce early booking volume 10–15% initially as price-sensitive customers shift to last-minute.
Failure condition: If deposit policy drives total booking volume below occupancy threshold, revenue loss from reduced volume exceeds cancellation savings. Mitigation: Apply deposit policy only to city hotels first — 41.7% cancel rate justifies it. Monitor resort separately.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Enforce 30% non-refundable deposit for all city hotel bookings with 60+ day lead time — highest cancel risk, highest revenue exposure.
Priority 2: Implement dynamic pricing surge for July–August — demand is 3× baseline. Flat pricing in peak season is leaving revenue on the table.
Priority 3: Build separate retention playbooks — city hotels (41.7%) and resort hotels (27.8%) are different problems with different solutions. Treating them the same will fix neither.

PROJECT 04
TechnoEdge Sales Analysis
Real Business Question
"Overall revenue is growing 34% — but leadership doesn't know if one region is carrying the entire business while others underperform. Are we allocating budget equally to regions that are not earning equally, and what would happen to total revenue if the top-performing region was replicated?"
Executive Summary
36+ monthly datasets were processed manually with no centralized view of which regions were driving growth vs. underperforming. North region delivered 42% of revenue on equal budget as weaker regions — the business was subsidising underperformers with top-performer capital. Decision: restructure regional budgets based on revenue-per-investment ratio, not headcount equality.
Problem"Why is revenue inconsistent across regions despite equal sales investment?" 36+ monthly datasets processed manually — no mid-month corrective action possible. Month-end reviews too late to course-correct underperforming regions.
ActionBuilt automated Power BI pipeline consolidating 36+ monthly datasets. DAX measures for YoY growth, regional revenue contribution, and product category performance. Drill-through from summary KPIs to transaction-level data in one click.
OutcomeNorth region: 42% of total revenue on equal investment as other regions. Bottom region identified — consuming budget without proportional output. Month-end blindspot eliminated. Mid-month corrective action now possible for sales leadership.
STOP: Equal budget allocation across all regions — it subsidises underperformers. Stop waiting for month-end reviews — mid-month data is now available and actionable.
Who benefits: Sales leadership (regional budget decisions) · Regional managers (performance visibility) · Finance team (budget reallocation approval)
Trade-off: Doubling down on North region increases revenue concentration risk — if one key account leaves, the impact is amplified.
Failure condition: If North's performance is relationship-driven (one key account) rather than structural — losing that account collapses the strategy. Mitigation: Audit North's customer concentration before budget shift — if top 2 accounts are more than 60% of North revenue, replication model is fragile.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Audit what North is doing differently — sales process, customer mix, pricing — and build a replication model for East region.
Priority 2: Set a 6-month performance deadline for the bottom region before budget reduction. Define the minimum revenue threshold explicitly — not a vague target.
Priority 3: Replace one national revenue target with region-specific targets tied to market size — national targets mask underperformers behind top-region numbers.

PROJECT 05
Pizza Sales Analysis — Menu & Staffing Optimisation
Real Business Question
"Our kitchen runs 3 full category lines at equal resource allocation across all hours — but profit margins are inconsistent. Which menu categories are consuming kitchen complexity without earning proportional revenue, and are we staffed correctly for when customers actually order?"
Executive Summary
Kitchen was running 3 category lines at equal priority across 48,620 orders. Classic category alone drove 53% (₹14.7L) of revenue while Veggie contributed only 8% (₹2.2L) — yet both received equal kitchen resources. Peak demand was concentrated at 12pm and 7pm with no staffing alignment. Decision: cut Veggie category complexity and realign 40% more staffing to peak windows.
Problem"Which menu items are we wasting kitchen resources on — and when should we actually be staffed up?" 3 category lines treated equally. No peak-hour staffing model. Ingredient inventory spread across all categories regardless of demand contribution.
ActionSQL analysis on 48,620 orders: revenue by category, size, and hourly time band using CTEs, window functions, and ranking queries. Identified peak order windows and daily staffing gaps. Revenue-per-category calculated per size variant.
OutcomeClassic: 53% of ₹27.7L total revenue on the simplest kitchen operation. Veggie: 8% revenue contribution — highest kitchen complexity per revenue rupee. Peak windows: 12pm and 7pm clearly identified. Staffing during these windows directly determines daily revenue ceiling.
STOP: Equal staffing across all hours — peak windows at 12pm and 7pm are where revenue is won or lost. Stop promoting Veggie variants — 8% revenue, high complexity. Stop treating all categories as strategic — Classic is the core, everything else is secondary.
Who benefits: Kitchen operations (staffing model) · Menu planning team (category review) · Procurement (ingredient concentration) · Shift managers (peak hour coverage)
Trade-off: Cutting Veggie category removes the only option for vegetarian customers — may trigger negative reviews in the first 30 days and reduce table covers.
Failure condition: If Classic category demand is bundled with Veggie in combo deals — removing Veggie breaks bundle revenue and reduces Classic orders simultaneously. Mitigation: Audit combo deal revenue before category cut. If Veggie combos are more than 15% of Classic orders, consolidate rather than cut.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Consolidate Veggie to 1 option maximum — reduce kitchen complexity while keeping vegetarian coverage. Audit if it's in combo deals first.
Priority 2: Add 40% more staff coverage at 12pm and 7pm peak windows — this is where daily revenue is determined, not averaged across the day.
Priority 3: Focus all promotions on Classic category — proven 53% revenue driver. Stop promoting low-performers — it trains customers to expect discounts on items that barely earn margin.

PROJECT 06
HR Analytics — Attrition Prediction & Retention Strategy
Real Business Question
"We are spending on recruitment every quarter while skilled employees keep leaving — and we only find out through exit interviews after the damage is done. Which departments and employee profiles are at highest attrition risk right now, and what signal in the data would have warned us 3 months earlier?"
Executive Summary
An organisation was losing 16.1% of 1,470 employees annually with no early warning system. Satisfaction data existed months before people quit — HR wasn't using it. R&D department and age 25–34 employees were the highest-risk segments. Decision: launch anonymous quarterly satisfaction checks in R&D immediately and link manager KPIs to team retention rate.
Problem"Why are we losing 16.1% of our workforce — and which departments are bleeding talent before we can act?" Exit interviews happen after the damage is done. HR decisions reactive, not preventive. Replacement cost per employee 6–9 months of salary — mounting with no strategy.
ActionAnalysed 1,470 employee records in Power BI across demographics, job roles, satisfaction scores, overtime, income, and tenure. Built attrition segmentation by department, age group, and satisfaction band. Created early warning view from satisfaction + overtime data combination.
OutcomeR&D department: highest attrition concentration. Age 25–34: most at-risk segment. Satisfaction score improvement correlates with 40% reduction in attrition probability — HR has a predictive signal and wasn't using it. Intervention possible 3–6 months before someone quits.
STOP: Reactive exit interviews — they happen after the cost is incurred. Stop equal retention investment across all departments — R&D needs immediate priority. Stop generic engagement surveys — anonymity is required for honest data.
Who benefits: HR department (intervention trigger system) · R&D department heads (retention programme) · C-suite (manager accountability framework)
Trade-off: Mandatory satisfaction surveys may feel performative without psychological safety — generating compliance responses, not honest signals.
Failure condition: If satisfaction surveys are not anonymous, employees will give safe answers — the early warning signal disappears and the entire strategy becomes a compliance exercise. Mitigation: Guarantee anonymity in writing before the first survey cycle. No manager-level visibility until aggregated to 5+ responses minimum.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Launch anonymous quarterly pulse checks in R&D immediately — highest attrition concentration, guaranteed anonymity mandatory.
Priority 2: Link manager performance reviews to team retention rate — make attrition a manager's problem, not only HR's problem.
Priority 3: Build a 90-day early warning flag from combined satisfaction score below threshold AND overtime hours above threshold — act before the resignation letter.

PROJECT 07
Zomato Restaurant Performance Analysis (SQL)
Real Business Question
"New restaurant partners are listing on Zomato and getting poor early ratings — which permanently suppresses their algorithmic visibility. Is there a pricing and cuisine combination that consistently produces higher ratings, and what is the exact price point above which customer satisfaction drops regardless of food quality?"
Executive Summary
New restaurant partners were listing at wrong price points and receiving poor early ratings — permanently damaging their Zomato ranking algorithm position. Fast Food at ₹180 average achieves 4.4★ while Premium at ₹650+ averages only 3.4★. Decision: cap new restaurant price guidance at ₹400–600 and fast-track Fast Food category onboarding.
Problem"Which restaurant types and price points actually maximise ratings and repeat orders — and which combinations guarantee poor early performance?" No pricing framework for new partners. Over-investment in premium positioning with data showing it underperforms.
ActionSQL multi-table joins, subqueries, GROUP BY aggregations, and window functions across restaurant, order, and rating tables. Analysed rating distributions by cuisine type, average cost band, and online delivery penetration. Identified ₹400 as the price-to-rating inflection point.
OutcomeFast Food: 4.4★ average rating at ₹180 average cost — highest ROI category. Premium (₹650+): 3.4★ average — rating deteriorates above ₹400 inflection point. 91% of Fast Food orders via online delivery — channel alignment confirmed. ₹400–600 identified as the pricing sweet spot across cuisines.
STOP: Recommending premium pricing (₹600+) to new restaurant partners — data shows ratings deteriorate above ₹400. Stop onboarding premium restaurants without additional support — they are the highest early churn risk.
Who benefits: New restaurant onboarding team (pricing playbook) · Zomato category managers (Fast Food growth brief) · Partner success team (early rating intervention programme)
Trade-off: Prioritising Fast Food category lowers GMV per order — higher volume at lower ticket size requires more orders to match premium revenue.
Failure condition: Rating data lags actual performance by 2–4 weeks — early onboarding decisions based on incomplete signals may be premature. Mitigation: Use 30-day rating trajectory, not just initial rating, before flagging a restaurant for intervention.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Cap all new restaurant partner pricing guidance at ₹400–600 sweet spot — above this, ratings deteriorate and early algorithm ranking suffers permanently.
Priority 2: Fast-track Fast Food category onboarding — 4.4★, ₹180, 91% online delivery. Highest rating ROI per rupee of operational support.
Priority 3: Flag all ₹600+ new listings for dedicated onboarding support before going live — without intervention, they risk poor early ratings that permanently suppress their algorithmic visibility.

PROJECT 08
Airbnb NYC — Pricing Strategy & Host Performance Analysis
Real Business Question
"Two identical Airbnb listings in the same NYC neighbourhood are earning 3× different revenue — and the higher-earning host is not necessarily offering better amenities. What is the single most controllable variable that separates top-earning hosts from average ones, and what is the exact pricing strategy a new host should follow from day one?"
Executive Summary
Identical Airbnb listings in the same NYC neighbourhood earned 3× different revenue — with no framework for hosts to understand why. Analysis of 48,895 listings revealed review frequency as the #1 controllable pricing lever: hosts with 10+ reviews/month commanded a 22% price premium over comparable listings. Decision: price at market rate for 60 days to accelerate reviews, then raise by 15–20%.
Problem"Why do identical listings in the same neighbourhood earn 3× different revenue — and what separates top-earning hosts from average ones?" New hosts pricing on gut feel. Manhattan listings earning $271/night while equivalent hosts charge $140. No data-based pricing framework available.
ActionPython EDA (Pandas, Seaborn, Matplotlib) on 48,895 NYC Airbnb listings. Neighbourhood-level price segmentation, room type distribution, availability patterns, and review frequency correlation with pricing power. Host performance comparison framework by borough and room type.
OutcomeReview frequency is the #1 controllable pricing lever — hosts with 10+ reviews/month command 22% premium. Manhattan entire-home: $271/night. Staten Island equivalent: $109 — $162 location premium confirmed. Entire home (52% of listings): $196/night avg vs Private Room (46%): $89/night.
STOP: Pricing on gut feel — Manhattan benchmark is $271/night for entire-home; underpricing means leaving $130+/night on the table. Stop over-investing in amenity upgrades before accumulating reviews — reviews earn the premium, not the coffee machine.
Who benefits: New Airbnb hosts (pricing playbook) · Airbnb growth team (host onboarding guidance) · Property managers (portfolio pricing optimisation)
Trade-off: Pricing at market rate for 60 days to accelerate reviews compresses initial revenue — the 60-day investment period earns less than full-price potential.
Failure condition: Strategy fails if the 60-day review accumulation period doesn't generate 10+ reviews/month — possibly due to seasonal low demand. Mitigation: Launch in Q4 or summer peak demand window — higher booking volume guarantees faster review accumulation.
Final Recommendation
Priority 1 (Do This First): Price at market rate for first 60 days — reviews beat revenue in the early stage. A 22% premium at month 3 outperforms flat pricing from day one.
Priority 2: After crossing 10 reviews/month, raise price 15–20% — the data supports this premium and the algorithm rewards it with higher search placement.
Priority 3: Location beats amenities — don't invest in premium upgrades if the neighbourhood benchmark is ₹109. Relocate capital to a higher-demand zone before spending on the listing itself.
